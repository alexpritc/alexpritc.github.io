Practice-Based Research Comparing Fantasy Short-Form Prose for Adults and Children


Task Summary

The non-exam assessment for the A-level English Language specification (AQA, 2017) was 'Language in Action', and consisted of an investigation, original writing and a commentary.

I had to produce two short stories (under 2000 words in total). These stories had to either:

  1. Share the same genre, but with different audiences.
  2. Or have different genres and audiences.

Each story had to be based on a model-writer and I had to decide which stylistic features my stories would share. These choices then had to be explained in a commentary (under 1000 words).


Research and Decision-Making

The genre I picked was is short-story fiction (specifically fantasy), as it was outside my comfort zone but I knew of some very interesting authors I could use as a reference model. I looked at authors such as Trudi Canavan, Douglass Adams, Neil Gaiman, Patrick Rothfuss, Diana Wynne Jones and Brandon Sanderson.

However, I decided to model first story (for adults) on Sir Terry Pratchett and my second (for children) on Daniel Morden.

The late Sir Terry Pratchett.

Welsh Author, Daniel Morden.

I decided the best way to emphasise how much target audiences influence the stylistic features of a story was to write two stories with the same genres, but one aimed at adults and the other aimed primarily at children (and secondarily at adults). This is because parents tend to read to children so that they learn the important morals and skills.

Having this scenario, where a parent is reading to their child, meant that I could have darker biblical references in the story that the adult would understand but the child would take no notice of; thus, making it enjoyable for both audiences. Since the genre and semantic field are the same, it allows me to demonstrate how I used different literary techniques to accommodate for each audience.


Specific Research-Points

"BEST OF THREE":

  • I wanted to use bathos, much like my style model (Terry Pratchett) does, in “BEST OF THREE” to build up tension and then reveal a much less impressive ending to create humour (adding to the entertaining purpose).
  • Another feature that I wanted to incorporate into “BEST OF THREE” is spelling phonetically (non-standard orthography) to convey accents in an almost transcript-like manner, as well as for humour.
  • Pratchett was known for using names that had no apparent connection to the plot, but were in fact spelt phonetically to disclose something about the storyline (e.g. “Schüschein” says “Shoeshine”). I adopted this to allude to the previous downfall of one of the characters hence the city name “Atrufi” (“Atrophy” spelt phonetically; which is a synonym of “Downfall”).
  • I will merge Pratchett's capitalisation of Death's speech with my own writing to distinguish between Death's and the mortal men's voices (e.g. “I HAVEN'T MUCH TO SAY ON THAT TOPIC.”) However, I will use it differently to Pratchett as in his novels; the reader tends to be aware that it is Death speaking, whereas in mine it is unknown to the reader until the end of the story.

"The Old Man":

  • “The Old Man” needs to follow a distinctive narrative structure (also present in Daniel Morden's 'Dark Tales from the Woods'), that uses collocations and discourse markers such as “Once upon a time” that children recognise.
  • I will include the anthropomorphic personification of Death, but in “The Old Man” this is extended to the Four Horsemen. In the drafting process, I did not treat “Pestilence”, for example, as a proper noun. However, I decided when discussing the personified version, it should be known as “Pestilence” rather than “disease” to emphasise this archaic feature of language and its importance when portraying a non-concrete concept.
  • This abstract concept has to be implied (Grice, 1980s) in “The Old Man” as it might be seen as ethically wrong by parents to expose their children to such grim ideologies. Therefore, referencing the Four Horsemen will be far subtler such as describing Famine as “a fat man”, which is a description that a child would be able to comprehend.

The Final Submission

You can view the .pdf files of all three sub-projects:

  • "BEST OF THREE", a short prose aimed at adults.
  • "The Old Man", a short prose aimed at primarily children, with the secondary audience of adults.
  • "Commentary", an essay disecting the features used in each story.

The overall project was graded 49/50.


Notes

This project taught me how to decide which language features are suitable for specific audiences, which is a really important skill for writers to have. The feedback I got throughout the 3 month process was incredibly useful and detailed, which helped build my confidence as someone who's only just begun writing (a level 1 writer).
